01 — The Problem

Western Cultural Bias in LLMs

Large Language Models trained predominantly on Western-centric internet data often perpetuate and amplify cultural biases. When deployed in diverse contexts like Pakistan, they can:

  • Marginalize local identities (Punjabi, Sindhi, Balochi, Pashtun)
  • Prioritize Western entities in names, food, dress, and traditions
  • Reduce cultural accuracy and inclusivity in AI systems
Why It Matters

Pakistan’s rich tapestry of four major cultures (Punjabi, Sindhi, Balochi, Pashtun) creates a perfect testbed for uncovering hidden Western dominance in LLMs.

02 — Research Purpose

Our Objective

To systematically investigate the presence and magnitude of Western cultural bias in seven open-source LLMs using culturally-specific prompts across Pakistan’s major cultures.

Key Contributions
  • Novel masked-cloze methodology tailored to Pakistani context
  • Evaluation across 10 cultural categories and 5 languages
  • Four rigorous bias & literacy metrics + Mann-Whitney U statistical tests
  • Actionable insights for culturally-aware AI development
03 — Methodology

Research Design

We employed a masked cloze-test framework comparing contextual (culturally grounded) vs. non-contextual prompts across 10 cultural categories in 5 languages.

Prompt Template: “In a traditional [Culture] household, the [MASK] is ...”

07 — Models Evaluated

Seven Open-Source LLMs

  • Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct – High reasoning, instruction-tuned (Alibaba)
  • Gemma-2-2B – Efficient Google model
  • Aya-Expanse-8B – Strong general-purpose (Cohere)
  • Qalb-1.0-8B-Instruct – Urdu-specialized (Llama 3.1 base, 1.97B Urdu tokens)
  • Llama-3.2-3B – Lightweight Meta model
  • Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3 – Efficient English + code
  • Lughaat-1.0-8B-Instruct – Urdu SOTA competitor (Muhammad Noman)
08 — Metrics

Four Key Metrics

\[ R = \frac{P(\text{Western})}{P(\text{Local})} \quad \text{(Bias Ratio)} \] \[ F_w = \frac{\text{Number of prompts with } R > 1}{\text{Total prompts}} \quad \text{(Western Preference Frequency)} \] \[ \text{NER Rate} = \text{Proportion of correctly identified local entities} \] \[ \Delta S = |P(\text{Local}) - P(\text{Western})| \quad \text{(Sentiment Difference)} \]

Statistical tests: Two-sided Mann-Whitney U on bias ratios (p < 0.05 significant).

11 — Model-by-Model Insights

Key Findings per Model

Mistral-7B (Best Performer)
• Near-zero Western bias (Fw ≈ 7.6%, R = 1.00)
• Strong local preference & highest stability
• Excellent NER in non-contextual settings
Qwen-2.5-7B (Worst Performer)
• Highest bias (Fw ≈ 53%, R ≈ 2.8)
• Extreme instability (Std up to 10⁶ scale)
• Bias barely reduced by context
Qalb-1.0-8B & Lughaat-1.0-8B (Urdu Models)
• Highest cultural literacy (NER up to 42%)
• Still show moderate Western bias (R > 1.2)
• Literacy does NOT eliminate bias

Key takeaway: Even Urdu-specialized models inherit Western priors; only Mistral shows genuine cultural neutrality.

12 — Key Insights

What the Data Reveals

  • Strongest Western bias in Male Names (Fw 55.51%, Bias Ratio 4.12) and Literature
  • Context reduces bias in 8/10 categories but never fully eliminates it
  • “Cultural Literacy Gap”: High NER recognition does NOT guarantee low bias (Qalb example)
  • Domestic categories (Food, Female Dress) show strongest local preference
  • Mistral-7B is the clear outlier with reverse/local bias
  • Larger instruction-tuned models (Qwen, Aya) exhibit highest instability
13 — Conclusions

Summary of Findings

Western cultural bias is systemic in current LLMs, especially in identity-related domains. Cultural context mitigates but does not eliminate the issue. Urdu-specialized models show higher literacy but similar bias patterns.

Core Takeaway

Recognizing a cultural entity does not guarantee fair treatment of that entity.
